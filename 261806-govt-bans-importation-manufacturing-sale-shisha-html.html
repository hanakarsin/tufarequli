Govt bans importation, manufacturing, sale and use of shisha

Publish date: 2024-04-03

- Through a legal notice published on Thursday, December 28, the government declared that no person shall import, manufacture, sell, offer for sale, use, advertise, promote or distribute shisha in Kenya

- The notice states that any person who contravenes any provisions of these rules may be liable to the penalty contemplated under section 163 of the Act

Kenya has banned use of shisha tobacco smoking effective immediately and anyone found enjoying the infamous water-pipe tobacco smoking will be punished.

Through a legal notice published on Thursday, December 28, Health Cabinet Secretary declared that no person shall import, manufacture, sell, offer for sale, use, advertise, promote or distribute shisha in Kenya.

"No person shall allow, promote, facilitate or encourage, or do anything to allow, promote, facilitate or encourage shisha smoking in Kenya," the legal notice further states.

According to the legal notice, any person who contravenes any provisions of these rules may, where a penalty has not been expressly provided for under any provision of the Act, be liable to the penalty contemplated under section 163 of the Act.

Shisha includes tobacco products that may be flavored or non-flavored and which are consumed using a single or multiple-stemmed smoking instrument that contain water and other liquids through which the smoke passes before reaching the smoker.

Shisha tobacco smoking is popular among the youth, especially young women going to night clubs and bars in Nairobi, Mombasa, and other major towns and cities in Kenya.

The move by the Kenyan government to ban shisha comes barely two weeks after Rwanda also banned the popular water-pipe tobacco smoking.

Rwanda banned use of shisha following a report by World Health Organization (WHO) that revealed smoke from the water pipe contained toxicants that caused lung cancer and heart diseases.

The WHO's report was corroborated by Centre for Diseases Control and Prevention (CDCP), which also found that the smoke emanating from the water pipe had toxics that were as harmful to human health as cigarette smoking.

The CDCP also linked shisha tobacco smoking to lung cancer, respiratory illness, low birth weight, and periodontal disease.

Kenya and Rwanda are not the first countries to ban use of shisha, also known as hookah.

Other countries where hookah smoking had been declared illegal include Tanzania, Saudi Arabia, Singapore, Malaysia, Jordan, and Pakistan.
